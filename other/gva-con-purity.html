Important information

Welcome to the Geneva Conventions Purity Test. The Four Geneva Conventions of 1949 and their Three Additional Protocols are a cornerstone of international humanitarian law. I have studied the subject for three weeks in New York and, after a suggestion from my good friend Eli, as well as a lot of time (javascript is intimidating guys I swear I'm not just lazy) I have created this test. A lot of this has been inspired by the summary of the Geneva Conventions written by myself and folks I worked with in New York at the time.

Obviously with everything going on in the world right now (this test has been published in summer of 2024) international humanitarian law violations are no joke. Let this test be a work of art which brings awareness to this crucial (but oftentimes ignored (by countries) and joked about (by iFunny users)) aspect of public international law. For more information, please check out the ICRC's Website. Thank you :)

As another warning, this test is most likely not exhaustive. More things will come up. In such a case, I will create an additional section after the additional protocols. I will of course increase the score. I am unable to efficiently verify 127 questions, so if there's a problem please reach out! I am sure a lot of people are better at spotting my mistakes than I am.

Here is how the test works. If you have done what is said in the checkbox, you check it. Interpret it as you wish. After all, law is an inexact science. Theoretically speaking, all of these boxes normally apply to IAC by default, but here if nothing is written about IAC or NIAC, presume it applies to both as objectively a breach is still a breach. The final score you're given is a score out of 127. As you check each box, the score updates live below. People who are completely clean have 0 out of 127. Have fun.

Glossary of abbreviations

GC1 stands for the First Geneva Convention for the Amelioration of the Condition of the Wounded and Sick in Armed Forces in the Field.
GC2 stands for the Second Geneva Convention for the Amelioration of the Condition of Wounded, Sick and Shipwrecked Members of Armed Forces at Sea.
GC3 stands for the Third Geneva Convention relative to the Treatment of Prisoners of War.
GC4 stands for the Fourth Geneva Convention relative to the Protection of Civilian Persons in Time of War.
AP1 stands for the First Additional Protocol relating to the Protection of Victims of International Armed Conflicts.
AP2 stands for the Second Additional Protocol relating to the Protection of Victims of Non-International Armed Conflicts.
AP3 stands for the Third Additional Protocol relating to the Adoption of an Additional Distinctive Emblem.
CA stands for common article.
art. stands for article.
IAC stands for international armed conflict.
NIAC stands for non-international armed conflict.
For more information on the meaning of certain words within this test, please scroll below to the "Dictionary" section. Thank you again!!!

Dictionary

Is considered hors de combat anybody injured and unable to continue to take part in combat.
Medical personnel means those persons assigned, by a Party to the conflict, exclusively to medical purposes.
Religious personnel means military or civilian persons, such as chaplains, who are exclusively engaged in the work of their ministry.
Shipwrecked means persons, whether military or civilian, who are in peril at sea or in other waters as a result of misfortune affecting them or the vessel or aircraft carrying them and who refrain from any act of hostility.
Wounded and sick categorizes people, military or civilian, injured or unstable mentally or physically, who need medical assistance.
As a rule of thumb, to check who is protected by the convention, look at the glossary of abbreviations and see what the convention protects. For example, for the Fourth Geneva Convention, protected persons are civilians. Normally everything you need to know should be within the test somewhere, if not, the internet is your friend.
