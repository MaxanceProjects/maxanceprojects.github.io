Difficulty table

A completely subjective classification of how difficult a hike is. No this isn't a climbing difficulty grading system, it's just for planning purposes. Each route has a difficulty rating. Depending on the rating you may need to prepare differently. What does this system take into account?

  • Distance
  • Time
  • Elevation change
  • Steepness
  • Average road (quality, width, etc.)
  • Average terrain (mainly quality)
  • Needed material
  • Needed skill
  • Level of risk
  • How tired someone is by the time they reach the end
  • Accessibility

Rating

Description

Example

1

Very Easy: requires little to no effort, a very casual walk. Great way to start going on longer walks if little to no experience.

Gouille Marion Walk (N1), Versoix Promenade (N8), Sentier des Pierres (NatuRando n°14)

2

Easy: can be done in half a day, can be a little bit longer with smaller path size. Differences in elevation are noticeable.

Chemin Spirituel d'Oujon (NatuRando n°6)

3

Fairly Easy: longer than a rating of 2, can be done anywhere between half a day and a full day.

Sentier de l'Arboretum (NatuRando n°29)

4

Light Medium: the average person may come back home fairly tired with a pain in the shins.

Sentier des Toblerones (NatuRando n°10)

5

Medium: more demanding than a rating of 4, terrain can be quite variable, elevation change is very noticeable, terrain can be fairly steep.

Crêt de la Neuve, Le Noirmont

6

Medium Hard: similar to a rating of 5, however the features are accentuated. A hike rated a 5 can become a 6 during the winter months, if not a 7. Terrain quality is significantly more variable, and steepness is accentuated, fatigue sets in quicker. Don't push your family beyond this rating :)

La Dôle, Mont Tendre, Le lac de Joux

7

Difficult: has a lot of mixed terrain and elevation change, takes a significant amount of time (about a full day). You will be exhausted, and not necessarily by the time you reach the end. Prepare well, eat well.

Dent de Lys, Tour d'Aï

8

Very Difficult: very demanding, prepare very well, you need to have had a fair bit of experience. It's like a rating of 7 but higher and longer.

Becca d'Audon, Wildstrubel

9

Intense: not only do you need experience and endurance, you might also need good strength, perfect planning and danger assessing skills, and a lot of maturity. You need experience, at the very worst, an error may or may not be fatal. Danger's the factor that separates this one from the 8.

Haute Cime, Dent d'Oche

10

Very Intense: just like rating 9, except more demanding, mainly because the paths are very small (not even on any map at times) - significantly more technical too! For experienced people only. Simply put, this is the border between hiking and alpinism.

Grand Muveran, Breithorn (one of the few 4000m exceptions), Sommet des Diablerets

11

Extreme: you need a whole load of experience from places rated 7 and 8, be qualified, this is very serious, it's dangerous. Have the correct gear. You'd also rather go with a guide. Anything above 4000m is at and beyond this limit. Preparation is key here. Couple days are required.

Mont Blanc (yes I know it's counterintuitive, but trust)

12

Very Extreme: similar to rating 11, except the conditions are more difficult, and there may be an even smaller border between you and death.

Dufourspitze, Finsteraarhorn, Dom

13

Severe: you've got yourself a really big achievement there, reserved for people with a tremendous amount of experience. A lot of planning has to go into it and also a lot of time has to be spent reaching the summit and back. Anything above 6k. A month in terms of time maybe? Idk I've never been.

Aconcagua

14

Extremely Severe: "Oh my God I'm staring into the eyes of the physical manifestation of death" - you get unlimited boasting rights. Above 8k, a.k.a the death zone.

Mount Everest, , K f****** 2

This is purely subjective, although it's fairly helpful for organizing a hike and knowing if it's fit for you and the folks you're bringing along. However, this doesn't take into account certain particularities or "quirks" of the place. For example, Le Noirmont is a place where you have to be mindful of herding/guard dogs in the area, this isn't taken into account. It just appeals to experience, knowledge, and physical capability. Put in a lot of planning before going somewhere, no matter where that somewhere is, don't do anything stupid even if the path is rated 1.

Anything above 10 is in white, not only so that you can see the text better, but because it gets into expert level waters. So basically, if you're the average hiker, you can use this scale out of 10 if you want to, and it won't make much of a difference (chances are you'll feel more accomplished when you get to that rating 10 because it's the top of the scale in a way).
